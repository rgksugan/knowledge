# Don't Sweat the Small Stuff ... and it's all small stuff
by [[Richard Carlson]]

## Highlights
> Live this day as if it were your last.

> As long as you think more is better, you’ll never be satisfied.

> The truth is, there’s no better time to be happy than right now.

> Liberals, conservatives—we’re all the same. We form opinions and then spend our entire lifetimes validating what we believe to be true.

> Criticism, like swearing, is actually nothing more than a bad habit.

> We cannot do great things on this earth. We can only do little things with great love.

> How many times have you criticized someone and had them respond by saying, “Thank you so much for pointing out my flaws. I really appreciate it”?

> When we judge or criticize another person, it says nothing about that person; it merely says something about our own need to be critical.

> Choose being kind over being right.

> If, on the other hand, you see each new issue you face as a serious battle that must be won in order to survive, you’re probably in for a very rocky journey.

> A low mood is not the time to analyze your life. To do so is emotional suicide.

> Surrender to the fact that life isn’t fair.

> If you don’t want to “sweat the small stuff,” it’s critical that you choose your battles wisely. If you do, there will come a day when you’ll rarely feel the need to do battle at all.

> If you want to be a more peaceful person you must understand that being right is almost never more important than allowing yourself to be happy.

> Being listened to and heard is one of the greatest desires of the human heart. And those who learn to listen are the most loved and respected. Those who are in the habit of correcting others are often resented and avoided.

> Many people, consciously or unconsciously, believe that it’s somehow their job to show others how their positions, statements, and points of view are incorrect, and that in doing so, the person they are correcting is going to somehow appreciate it, or at least learn something. Wrong!

> Life is what’s happening while we’re busy making other plans.

> Remind yourself (before a conversation begins, if possible) to be patient and wait. Tell yourself to allow the other person to finish speaking before you take your turn.

> Change the things that can be changed, accept those that cannot, and have the wisdom to know the difference.

> The need for perfection and the desire for inner tranquility conflict with each other.

> The solution here is to catch yourself when you fall into your habit of insisting that things should be other than they are. Gently remind yourself that life is okay the way it is, right now. In the absence of your judgment, everything would be fine.

> As you begin to eliminate your need for perfection in all areas of your life, you’ll begin to discover the perfection in life itself.
