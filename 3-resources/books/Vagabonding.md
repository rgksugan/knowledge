# Vagabonding
by [[Rolf Potts]]

## Highlights
> And the more we associate money with life, the more we convince ourselves that we’re too poor to buy our freedom.

> Cities are full of those who have been caught in monthly payments for avocado green furniture sets.

> In fact, entire multinational backpacker circuits (not to mention budget-guidebook publishing empires) have been created by the simple abundance of such travel bargains in the developing world.

> Vagabonding is not just a process of discovering the world but a way of seeing—an attitude that prepares you to find the things you weren’t looking for.

> The discoveries that come with travel, of course, have long been considered the purest form of education a person can acquire.

> For Americans, the European circuit is an instinctive vagabonding destination, but nearly any part of the world can be travel-friendly. Should you want to get the most out of your travel dollar, Southeast Asia, the Indian subcontinent, the Middle East, Central America, and South America are all home to cheap, safe, time-honored vagabonding circuits.

> If possible, go on short road trips with your potential partner before you go vagabonding together; it’s amazing what you can learn about your compatibility in just a couple of days.

> Peculiar travel suggestions are dancing lessons from God.

> In a certain sense, walking through new places with the instincts of a five-year-old is liberating.

> Indeed, one of the big clichés of modern travel is the fear of letdown at a place you’ve always dreamed of visiting.

> In other words, tourist attractions are defined by their collective popularity, and that very popularity tends to devalue the individual experience of such attractions.

> Thus, on the road, you should never forget that you are uniquely in control of your own agenda.

> People travel to faraway places to watch, in fascination, the kind of people they ignore at home.

> If you can cook it, boil it, or peel it, you can eat it—otherwise, forget it.

> It is so often circumstance that makes an adventure, not a place or an action.

> Of all the adventures and challenges that wait on the vagabonding road, the most difficult can be the act of coming home.

> On a certain level, coming home will be a drag because it signals the end of all the fun, freedom, and serendipity that you enjoyed on the road.

> In trying to make sense of this homecoming experience, people often quote T. S. Eliot’s “Little Gidding”: And the end of all our exploring Will be to arrive where we started.

> One of the most difficult things I experienced in my travels was trying to relate what I’d experienced to old friends and acquaintances who’d been at home the whole time I was gone.

> Explore your hometown as if it were a foreign land, and take an interest in your neighbors as if they were exotic tribesmen.
